Indigenous Lands - Their importance comes from the past

The environmental conservation of Indigenous Lands is a strategy of territorial occupation established by indigenous peoples. Indigenous peoples help to expand the diversity of fauna and of the local flora because they have unique ways of living and occupying a place.

Recent research has shown that indigenous peoples played a fundamental role in shaping of the biodiversity found in South America. Many plants, for example, emerged as product of indigenous forest management techniques, such as chestnut, peach palm, cocoa, babassu, cassava and araucaria. In the case of Brazil nuts and Araucaria, these trees would have been distributed over a large area by indigenous peoples before European occupation in continent.

These people's management of biodiversity played a fundamental role in the formation of different landscapes in Brazil, whether in the Amazon, the Cerrado, the Pampa, the Atlantic Forest, the Caatinga, or in the Pantanal. Indigenous peoples have always used natural resources without putting their ecosystems. These people have developed appropriate forms of management that have proven to be very important for the conservation of biodiversity in Brazil. This management included the transformation from the poor soil of the Amazon into a very fertile type, Terra Preta de Índio. It is estimated that at less than 12% of the total surface of the Amazon soil had its characteristics transformed by man in this process.

In southern Brazil, for example, TI Mangueirinha helps conserve one of the last forests of araucaria native to the world, while in the south of Bahia, the PataxódaTI BarraVelha, help to protect one of the remaining areas of greatest biodiversity in the Atlantic Forest. In the Amazon, largest Brazilian biome, while 20% of the forest has already been deforested in the last 40 years, together Indigenous Lands lost just 2.4% of their original forests.

And it is not just for the conservation of forests that indigenous peoples and their territories are important. At a time when scientists are drawing attention to the decline of biodiversity and the diversity of cultivated plants, agro biodiversity has indigenous peoples as faithful guardians. The Alto Rio Negro is a great center of diversity of cultivated plants, and the agricultural system of the Indians in this region was recognized by the Institute of National Historical and Artistic Heritage (Iphan) as Cultural Heritage of Brazil.

The list of products from this agro-biodiversity is quite extensive, many plants present today on our tables were presented to us by the agricultural systems of indigenous peoples. Açaí, peanuts, several species of potatoes and peppers, as well as a large amount of corn and bean seeds, to name a few.

It is not only about cultivated plants that the knowledge of indigenous peoples extends. It's because This is what scientists linked to the Intergovernmental Platform for Biodiversity and Services Ecosystems (Ipbes) have focused on the role of indigenous knowledge on biodiversity. Ipbes is an international entity created in 2012 and has, among its principles, recognize the contribution of indigenous knowledge to the conservation and use sustainability of biodiversity and ecosystems.

The importance of Indigenous Lands in the conservation of biodiversity forced the formulation of a legal framework to promote the environmental management of indigenous territories, through the Policy National Territorial and Environmental Management of Indigenous Lands (PNGATI). Today, the great struggle of indigenous peoples is so that the set of laws derived from this recognition is fulfilled, ensuring the maintenance of their lands free from invaders and in environmental conditions that allow them to live according to their ways of life.
