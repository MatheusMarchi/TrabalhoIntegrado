Cultural Legacy of indigenous: Customs, Cuisine, Folklore among other things

When we think about the first settlers of Brazil, the arrival of the Portuguese comes to mind. But this is changing, as in some textbooks we already have the history of indigenous peoples as the first populations that inhabited Brazilian territory.

From the year 1500, when Europeans arrived, to the present day, the indigenous population decreased drastically, from three to five million Indians to, currently, according to FUNAI (National Indian Foundation), 358 thousand Indians.

Even after indigenous peoples have gone through the process of conquest and extermination, they They left us with different cultural practices. Demonstrate some of these practices present in our society will be our objective in this text.

According to Brazilian folklore, there was the legend of the curupira (inhabitant of the forests Brazilian communities), whose main task would be to protect animals and plants. Always recurring in legends, the curupira had its feet with heels forward to confuse hunters. According to historian Sérgio Buarque de Holanda, the curupira did not exist, but the indigenous people They had a habit of walking backwards, to confuse Europeans and bandeirantes..

The desire to go barefoot was another habit we inherited from the indigenous people. Generally, when When we get home after a full day of work or study, the first thing we do is Remove your shoes and remain barefoot for a while. Many people have the habit of always walking barefoot when they are in their homes.

The custom of resting in hammocks is another heritage of indigenous peoples. Almost always the Indians They sleep in straw hammocks located inside their huts (their homes in the villages).

Brazilian cuisine inherited several habits and customs from indigenous culture, such as the use of cassava and its derivatives (cassava flour, beiju, starch), the custom of eating fish, meat pounded in a wooden pestle (known as paçoca) and dishes derived from game (such as alligator mince and tucupi duck), in addition to the custom of eating fruit (mainly cupuaçu, bacuri, soursop, cashew, açaí and buriti).

In addition to the indigenous influence on Brazilian cuisine, we also inherited the belief in traditional practices popular healing methods derived from plants. That's why we always use guaraná powder, boldo, copaíba oil, catuaba, sucupira seed, among others, to cure any illness.

The indigenous cultural influence on Brazilian society does not stop there: the Portuguese language Brazilian language was also influenced by indigenous languages. Several words of indigenous origin are found in our everyday vocabulary, such as words linked to flora and fauna (such as pineapple, caju, cassava, armadillo) and words that are used as proper nouns (such as parque do Ibirapuera, in São Paulo, which means “place that was once forest”, where “ibira” means tree and “puera” have the meaning of something that once was. The Tietê River in São Paulo is also a name indigenous meaning “true river”).

Indigenous peoples left Brazilian society with a cultural diversity that was important for the formation of the Brazilian population.
