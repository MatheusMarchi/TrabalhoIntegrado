Indigenous history before and after European arrival: How it was, theories and records

Already inhabited, Brazil became a destination for Portuguese explorers who found a population indigenous people devoid of any written history. The extent of our knowledge about these native Brazilians during the 1500s was largely due to European visitors who documented their encounters - it is through these accounts that we piece together their way of life. Some notable accounts include those by Hans Staden, a German author, and Jean de Lery, a French, who resided among these Brazilians in the mid-16th century.

Relating details that range from the most mundane, such as clothes and adornments, to the most complexes, such as religious beliefs, both give insight into the indigenous way of life. Anthropology and archeology were crucial in clarifying the historical periods before the arrival Portuguese. Despite the gaps, a holistic understanding was possible through the contribution of these fields.

Dating from 16,000 BC, 14,200 BC and 12,770 BC, archaeological excavations in Lagoa Santa (MG), Rio Claro (SP) and Ibicuí (RS) show evidence of human beings in Brazil. The colonization of South America, according to most researchers, began about 20,000 years ago. Although the species spread throughout the national territory around 9,000 BC, this was consequence of a significant increase in the human population.

Throughout this process, linguistic and social differentiation would have occurred that gave rise to the Macro-Jê and Macro-Tupi indigenous trunks. From the latter, between the 8th and 9th centuries, the Tupi and Guarani nations. They are the ones that stand out most in the last 500 years of Brazilian History, precisely because they had closer contact with the white man.

Upon the arrival of Pedro Álvares Cabral in 1500, it is estimated that Brazilian Indians were between a and five million. The Tupi occupied the coastal region that stretches from Ceará to Cananeia (SP). You Guaranis were spread along the southern coast of the country and the interior area, in the basin of the Paraná and Paraguay. In other regions, there were other tribes, generically called tapuias, Tupi word that designates indigenous who speak another language.

Despite the geographical division, the Tupi and Guarani societies were quite similar to each other, in linguistic and cultural aspects. Groups formed and stayed together mainly through kinship ties, which also articulated the relationship between these same groups among themselves. Smaller groups, villages were linked through kinship with units larger, the tribes.
