Miscegenation in Brazil: Data and Information about

Three in every five Brazilians, the equivalent of around 95 million people, carry in each cell of the body a genetic heritage that comes from Indians or Africans and has been preserved by through mothers over generations. Even if it doesn't necessarily translate into facial features or skin color, miscegenation among the indigenous peoples of Brazil is more intense than if imagined, as demonstrated by a group of researchers from the Federal University of Minas Gerais (UFMG) in an article published in Ciência Hoje in April.

The team, coordinated by geneticist Sérgio Danilo Pena, analyzed the genetic formation of population based on the DNA of 200 white Brazilians, from different origins and regions of the country. As biological markers, researchers examined Y chromosome variation, exclusively male. In women, they studied mitochondrial DNA, found in mitochondria, an organelle of the cell, and considered one of the most accurate indicators of inheritance maternal (paternal mitochondria are lost during fertilization of the egg).

It was found that around 60% of matrilineages (maternal lineages) are of Amerindian or African, while the majority, more than 90%, of the patrilineages of white Brazilians are from European origin – therefore, only one in ten white Brazilians has a paternal ancestor black or Indian. However, on the maternal side, six in ten have black or Indian ancestry. Maternal inheritance, specifically, is made up of 33% of Amerindian lineages, 28% of African and 39% European, with variations from region to region. In the South, the lineage predominates European, and in the Northeast, African.

“Although since 1500 the number of natives in Brazil has been reduced to 10% of the original (about 3.5 million to 325 thousand), the number of people with Amerindian mitochondrial DNA increased by about 10 times”, report the research authors. Patrilineages, very similar to the distribution genetics verified in Portugal, exhibit a variability that reflects the formation of the people Iberian, to which Celts, Phoenicians, Greeks, Romans, Jews and, especially, contributed Moors, who occupied Portuguese territory for 700 years.

Genetics complements historical and sociological studies on miscegenation, which began when the Portuguese arrived – and, alone, established informal relations with the Indian women. O study also questions the term race. For scientists, it is scientifically inconsistent, yet widely adopted, and should be replaced by ethnicity. According to the researchers, “there is no There is no biologically (nor culturally) homogeneous human group on Earth.”
